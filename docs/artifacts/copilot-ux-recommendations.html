The copilot, made coherent

Six questions about how the AI copilot is summoned, expanded, and framed — answered with a recommendation on each, grounded in the current code. Nothing here is built yet. This is a decision doc: read the verdicts, tell me which you want, and I'll implement.

One agent · two surfaces Honors Direction v2 (D2, D5) 6 decisions + 1 bug found

The doctrine we're keeping — the copilot is one agent in two frames: a right-docked co-working rail (quick, page-aware, summonable) and a full-page threaded chat (book-wide, browsable history). Both are ratified in DIRECTION-v2. Every recommendation below protects that split while removing the friction between the two.

01

How many ways to summon the rail?

Right now: a labelled pill in the nav footer, a floating button bottom-right, and ⌘K — all calling the same toggleChat(). Three doors to one room.

The standard

Products with a docked assistant give it one persistent visual trigger plus a keyboard shortcut — never two competing buttons. A floating action button is the norm for chat widgets (Intercom, Drift), but in a dense analytics tool it floats over your data — in the screenshots it's sitting on the risk-watchlist score.

  • Two persistent buttons → "which one do I press?" ambiguity.
  • The FAB occludes tiles; the pill is labelled & never covers content.
Recommended
page — unobstructed
dock

One labelled "Ask Bricklayer" pill + ⌘K. Nothing floats over data.

Recommend

Keep one persistent trigger: the labelled "Ask Bricklayer" pill in the nav (it's discoverable, on-brand, and never covers a chart). Remove the floating button. Keep ⌘K working as the invisible power path — it opens the copilot — but don't print the shortcut in the UI. It's a standard convention users already know (Cursor, Linear, Raycast), so a badge just adds chrome. Removing the stray ⌘K hint on the search field also disposes of the inconsistency above in one move: no visible claim, no false promise to reconcile.

If you love the floating button instead, the inverse is fine — keep the FAB, drop the pill — but then move it so it never overlaps tile content. The rule is one, not the specific one.

02

"Expand to full page" shouldn't feel like leaving

The instinct is right — it feels weird because the app does two jarring things at once: the rail disappears, and the whole app navigates. Your brain reads that as "I lost my chat," even though the thread technically carries over in state.

Recommend

Keep the promote action (it's a ratified capability), but fix the transition:

  • Land on the live thread, never the empty hero. The single biggest cause of the "did I lose it?" feeling. Guarantee the full page opens on the conversation you were just in.
  • Animate the hand-off. Widen the rail into the full-page layout (or a quick cross-fade) so it reads as growth, not a close-and-relaunch. No flash of empty white between.
  • Keep the composer's draft & scroll position. If they'd half-typed a question, it should survive the jump.

Net: "expand" becomes "give me more room for this," which is exactly the mental model of the two-surface doctrine.

03

Does Chat belong in the side nav?

Yes — but the reason the question comes up is real. Today the two entrances feel like duplicates because they behave differently: the nav lands you on a fresh empty page, "expand" promotes a thread. Same destination, two personalities.

Why keep it

  • Thread history needs a browsable home — you can't get "my past analyses" from a transient rail.
  • It's the surface for long, book-wide work where the 400px rail is too cramped.
  • It's ratified (Direction v2, D2) — full-page chat is a first-class destination.
  • It matches every AI product's mental model (ChatGPT/Claude): a place chat lives, plus an assistant that visits other pages.

The two jobs, named

The rail — quick, in-context, "answer about this page." Summoned, disposable, 400px.

The page — deliberate, book-wide, threaded, with history. A destination you go to.

If a user can articulate this split, the nav item stops feeling redundant. Right now the product doesn't tell them.

Recommend

Keep the Chat destination. Resolve the redundancy not by deleting it, but by making the two entrances consistent: both land in the identical full-page layout (decision #4), and the nav entry opens your most-recent thread (or a clear "new/recent" split) rather than always dumping you on the empty hero. The nav item is the front door; "expand" is the same room reached from inside the rail.

04

Two side navs — and should the main one step aside?

This is the crux. Yes, two rails is a legitimate, common pattern — when the second one is a list pane, not a second global menu. Gmail (app rail + folders), Slack (workspaces + channels), Linear (nav + issue list), and ChatGPT-in-a-shell all do exactly this. Your threads rail is a list pane, so the pattern is sound.

The problem isn't two rails — it's that both are at full width, so the main nav's labels compete with the thread list for the same job (navigation) and eat the horizontal room.

Recommend

On /chat, auto-collapse the main app nav to its 72px icon rail and let the threads rail breathe. This is the "focused workspace" convention (Slack/Gmail/Linear).

Make it route-driven, not entry-driven — and that single choice dissolves your worry. You noticed it'd feel weird if the collapse depended on how you arrived (rail-expand vs nav-click). So don't tie it to the entrance: tie it to being on /chat. Every path in produces the identical layout — collapsed icon nav + threads rail + conversation. Nothing jumps.

  • User can manually re-expand the main nav if they want it back — their override wins and persists.
  • Leaving /chat restores their normal nav state everywhere else.
  • The icon rail keeps all five destinations one click away — global nav is never trapped behind the chat.
Why not zero navs?
Hiding the main nav entirely on /chat would make chat feel like a separate app and strand the user. The icon rail is the sweet spot: present, quiet, out of the way.
05

The header borders don't line up

The only offender is the docked copilot header at 52px — so its bottom hairline never meets the continuous line the rest of the shell shares.

Yes, fix it. Set the docked copilot header to 60px to meet the shared line. While in there, promote 60px to a single --header-h token that all four headers (top bar, nav, dock, threads rail) reference — so they can never silently drift apart again.

06

Is the copilot's colour right?

The hue is right — the copilot wears the Bricklayer brand indigo→violet, and it should. Giving the AI its own colour would fragment the identity and imply it's a bolted-on third-party thing. Keep the brand. The issue is treatment hierarchy: the full multi-stop gradient is currently painted on ~5 things on a single screen — the FAB, the nav pill, the dock header square, the empty-state hero, and the thread-rail badge. Repeated that often, the "special" gradient stops feeling special, and it out-shouts the rest of the app (whose interactive elements use the flat indigo --primary).

Proposed treatment ladder

Gradient — the brand moment
The one summon control + the empty-state hero. Max once or twice per screen.
Solid indigo — small identity
Dock header badge, thread-rail badge, in-message avatar. Uses --primary #653ed3 like the rest of the app.
Tinted / mono — quiet chrome
Tiny or repeated glyphs where even solid indigo is too loud.
Recommend

Keep the hue; introduce a hierarchy. Reserve the --brand-gradient-action for the single summon affordance and the empty-state hero — the genuine "enter the AI" brand moments. Downgrade the small, repeated identity glyphs (dock header, thread badge, message avatars) to solid --primary indigo or a soft tint, so they match how every other interactive element in the app is coloured. The gradient becomes punctuation, not wallpaper — and the copilot stops looking like it's on a different design system than the tool it lives in.

Keep the brick-courses emblem itself — it's on-brand and distinctive; this is purely about the fill behind it.
